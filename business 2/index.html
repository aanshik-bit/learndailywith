How to Stay Protected in the Summer Heat

Summer heat isn't just uncomfortable — prolonged exposure to high temperatures can be genuinely dangerous. Whether you're dealing with a dry heat wave or humid sticky days, this guide covers everything you need to stay safe, healthy, and comfortable.

Understanding Heat and Its Risks

Before protection, it helps to understand what heat actually does to your body. Your core temperature normally sits around 37°C (98.6°F). When the environment heats up, your body works hard to regulate it — primarily through sweating and increased blood flow to the skin.

When these mechanisms are overwhelmed, things go wrong quickly:

  • Heat cramps — painful muscle spasms from electrolyte loss
  • Heat exhaustion — heavy sweating, weakness, cold/pale/clammy skin, faint pulse, nausea
  • Heat stroke — body temperature above 40°C (104°F), hot/red/dry skin, rapid strong pulse, possible unconsciousness. This is a medical emergency.

Knowing these signs in yourself and others can be life-saving.

Hydration: Your First Line of Defense

Dehydration accelerates every heat-related condition. In peak summer heat, your body can lose over a litre of water per hour through sweat alone.

How much to drink: The "8 glasses a day" rule is a rough baseline. In high heat, especially if you're active, aim for 3–4 litres daily. A simple check — your urine should be pale yellow.

What to drink:

  • Water is always your best option
  • Coconut water and ORS (oral rehydration salts) help if you've been sweating heavily — you need electrolytes (sodium, potassium), not just water
  • Buttermilk, lemon water with a pinch of salt, and fresh fruit juices are excellent traditional options
  • Avoid alcohol, excessive caffeine, and heavily sugary drinks — they increase dehydration

When to drink: Don't wait until you're thirsty. By the time thirst kicks in, you're already mildly dehydrated. Sip consistently throughout the day.

Dressing for the Heat

Clothing is a critical and often overlooked layer of protection.

Fabric choices:

  • Cotton and linen — breathable, absorb sweat, allow air circulation
  • Moisture-wicking synthetics — great for exercise, pull sweat away from skin
  • Avoid dark colours — they absorb more solar radiation; light colours reflect it
  • Avoid synthetic fabrics that trap heat like polyester in non-wicking forms

Fit matters:

Loose, flowing clothes allow airflow around your body. Tight clothing traps heat and can cause rashes.

Head and neck protection:

  • A wide-brimmed hat protects your face, neck, and ears — areas prone to sunburn
  • A light cotton scarf or dupatta can shield the neck and shoulders
  • Avoid metal accessories in direct sun — they heat up fast and can burn skin

Managing Sun Exposure

The sun is the primary source of summer heat stress and skin damage.

Peak hours: UV radiation and heat are most intense between 11 AM and 4 PM. Avoid direct outdoor exposure during this window when possible, especially physical activity.

Sunscreen:

  • Use SPF 30 or higher; SPF 50+ if you're fair-skinned or spending extended time outdoors
  • Reapply every 2 hours, more often if sweating or swimming
  • Don't forget ears, back of neck, tops of hands, and feet
  • Apply 20–30 minutes before going out

Shade strategy:

  • Trees offer better shade than structures — they also cool the surrounding air through transpiration
  • When walking, move through shaded routes even if they're slightly longer

Cooling Your Environment

Your home or workspace becomes your refuge. Making it cool is worth the effort.

Passive cooling techniques:

  • Close curtains and blinds on sun-facing windows during the day — this alone can reduce indoor temperature significantly
  • Open windows in the early morning (before 8 AM) to let cool air in, then close them before the heat builds
  • Hang wet curtains or use khus (vetiver) screens — traditional and surprisingly effective

Active cooling:

  • Ceiling fans combined with a wet cloth on a tray of ice can cool a room cheaply
  • Air conditioners work best when doors and windows are sealed — don't let cool air escape
  • Set your AC between 24–26°C; extremely cold settings increase the shock when you go outside and raise electricity bills

Body cooling:

  • Cool (not ice-cold) showers help — cold shocks can cause vasoconstriction and actually raise core temperature
  • Wet wrists, behind the knees, and the neck are pulse points — cooling them cools blood quickly
  • A damp cloth on the forehead provides immediate relief

Diet in Summer

What you eat significantly affects how your body handles heat.

Foods to favour:

  • High-water-content fruits and vegetables: watermelon, cucumber, muskmelon, tomatoes, oranges, mint
  • Light, easily digestible meals — your body uses less energy processing them, generating less internal heat
  • Curd, buttermilk, and lassi — probiotic, cooling, and hydrating
  • Aam panna (raw mango drink) — rich in electrolytes and traditionally trusted for heat protection

Foods to limit:

  • Spicy food raises body temperature and increases sweating — limit during peak heat hours
  • Heavy, oily, and fried foods generate more internal heat during digestion
  • Reduce red meat consumption on very hot days

Meal timing: Eat smaller, more frequent meals. A heavy meal raises your metabolic heat. Main meals are better in the cooler parts of the day — morning and evening.

Physical Activity and Outdoor Work

  • Schedule exercise or physical work for early morning (before 9 AM) or after sunset
  • Take frequent breaks in the shade — even 10 minutes of rest significantly reduces heat accumulation
  • Never ignore warning signs: dizziness, headache, nausea, or sudden fatigue are signals to stop immediately and cool down
  • Work at a slower pace — rushing in heat is dangerous
  • Never leave children, elderly people, or pets in parked vehicles — temperatures inside a parked car can exceed 60°C within minutes

Protecting Vulnerable Groups

Some people need extra attention during heat waves:

  • Elderly individuals: Their ability to sense heat and regulate body temperature declines with age. Check on elderly family members and neighbours daily. Ensure they're drinking enough water even if they say they're not thirsty.
  • Children: Children generate more body heat relative to their size and dehydrate faster. Keep them indoors during peak hours, ensure regular fluid intake, and watch for signs of heat exhaustion.
  • People with chronic illness: Conditions like heart disease, kidney disease, and diabetes, as well as medications like diuretics and antihistamines, can impair heat tolerance. Consult a doctor about additional precautions.
  • Outdoor workers: Construction workers, farmers, and delivery workers face the highest risk. Employers have a responsibility to provide shade, water, and rest periods.

Emergency Response

If someone shows signs of heat stroke — confusion, loss of consciousness, hot dry skin, rapid strong pulse:

  • Call emergency services immediately
  • Move them to a cool, shaded place
  • Cool them as rapidly as possible — wet cloth, fan, or immersion if available
  • Do not give fluids to an unconscious person
  • Monitor breathing until help arrives

Heat stroke can be fatal within minutes if untreated. Act fast.

Summer heat is manageable — even intense heat — with the right habits and awareness. The body is remarkably adaptive, but it needs your help. Stay consistent with these practices, and the season becomes far more survivable and even enjoyable.

Stay cool. Stay safe.
